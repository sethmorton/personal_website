# Surprise has shape

In 1867, a young [Hermann von Helmholtz](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hermann-helmholtz/) walked past the garrison chapel in Potsdam and looked up at the belfry. He saw a row of small figures standing in the tower and asked his mom to reach up and grab one. He thought they were dolls. Turns out, they were adults just standing far away.

---

Decades later, he'd use that memory to make a deeper point about [how perception actually works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconscious_inference): the retinal image is ambiguous. The data coming into the eye doesn't arrive labeled, and the brain has to guess what it is. Most of the time it guesses right. Sometimes it guesses dolls.

Helmholtz's insight was that the brain isn't passively receiving reality - it's running a model of what's out there and checking that model against incoming data. A wrong guess isn't just a mistake, it acts as information. If the world comes back in a way the model didn't expect, that mismatch tells the brain something about how the model needs to change.

By surprise in this essay, I mean something specific: the mismatch between what a system's model predicted and what actually showed up. Not the feeling of being startled. The structured gap between expectation and reality.

But that raises a question. If the brain is always making predictions and always getting some of them wrong, what tells it which errors matter? Which part of the model to update, and by how much?

That question has been sitting underneath a lot of work in both cognition and ML. Helmholtz gave the intuition. [Schmidhuber](https://people.idsia.ch/~juergen/ieeecreative.pdf) was the first to build it into an artificial system (1991, Swiss AI Lab IDSIA). He made an agent that rewarded itself whenever its predictions improved, so the learning signal came from the system's own dynamics rather than an external teacher. [Rao and Ballard](https://www.nature.com/articles/nn0199_79) then showed in 1999 that the brain actually does something like this in visual cortex: higher areas send predictions downward, lower areas send back the errors, and the mismatch drives learning at each level of the hierarchy. [Friston](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn2787) generalized the whole picture in 2010 under the Free Energy Principle, arguing that any self-organizing system works to minimize its own prediction error over time.

What all of these share: biological systems have built-in mechanisms for turning prediction failure into a learning signal. No human has to label the mistake, no reward function has to be designed. If you could build that into an artificial system, you wouldn't need humans standing behind the model forever deciding what counts as progress. The system would generate its own reason to learn.

People have been trying to build this for over thirty years. It keeps failing, and it keeps failing in the same way. I think I know why.

> **TLDR:** We keep collapsing surprise into a single number before asking what should change. I think that's the wrong move, and I think a settling-based substrate is how you fix it.

## The noisy TV

In 2018, [a team at OpenAI](https://pathak22.github.io/large-scale-curiosity/) built an agent driven entirely by prediction error and dropped it into a set of 3D environments. The agent tried to predict what it would see next, and whenever the prediction was wrong, that wrongness became reward. In many environments this worked beautifully, with the agent exploring rooms, solving mazes, and even discovering [secrets in Super Mario Bros](https://openai.com/index/reinforcement-learning-with-prediction-based-rewards/).

Then the researchers put a TV in one of the rooms, playing random static.

The agent stopped exploring - it just sat there, glued to the screen.

Random pixels are impossible to predict, so prediction error stays high forever. Since prediction error is the reward, the agent had found an infinite jackpot: something that looked maximally interesting while teaching it absolutely nothing. This became known as the [noisy TV problem](https://openai.com/index/reinforcement-learning-with-prediction-based-rewards/), and it's not an edge case. Every serious attempt to build prediction-error-based learning has hit some version of this - the system can't tell the difference between something hard to predict because it has structure and something hard to predict because it's noise.

A lot of clever work has gone into trying to fix this.

## The attempts

The earliest effort came in 2017, before the noisy TV was formally named. [Pathak et al. at UC Berkeley](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.05363) proposed the [Intrinsic Curiosity Module](https://pathak22.github.io/noreward-rl/), which changed what the agent predicts. Instead of predicting raw pixels, it learns a compressed representation that only captures things the agent can influence - focus on the door handle and the light switch, ignore the shadows shifting on the wall. Prediction error gets computed in that filtered space, so uncontrollable noise should get screened out. It helped, but in stochastic enough environments noise still leaked through.

After the noisy TV was formally demonstrated, the same OpenAI team proposed [Random Network Distillation](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.12894), which changed the approach entirely. Instead of predicting what happens next, RND rewards the agent for encountering unfamiliar states. Familiar states become easy for the network to process, novel states stay hard. This sidestepped the noisy TV in deterministic environments, but when the randomness depends on the agent's own actions it breaks down. The agent keeps encountering states that feel new but are really just fresh rolls of the same stochastic dice.

[Oudeyer](http://www.pyoudeyer.com/) and colleagues developed what's probably the most principled approach: [learning progress](http://www.pyoudeyer.com/oudeyerGottliebLopesPBR16.pdf), which asks not "what is surprising?" but "what am I getting better at predicting?" If error is decreasing, keep paying attention; if it's flat, move on. But when an agent encounters something new and error spikes, it can't tell at the moment of contact whether this is learnable structure or random noise. It has to wait and measure, and the measurement window is itself a design choice that can go wrong in both directions.

Each of these partially works. But the field keeps trying to solve the problem by being smarter about the input - what to predict, what to represent, when to care - without questioning the output. The criterion that decides whether an experience is worth learning from always gets compressed into a single number.

A single number can tell you how wrong you were. It can't tell you what kind of wrong.

## The scalar collapse

I call this the scalar collapse. And it's not just a quirk of curiosity papers; the same pattern shows up in the theoretical foundations. [Rao and Ballard's](https://www.nature.com/articles/nn0199_79) original cortical model has genuinely distributed error: different brain areas carry different local mismatches, the error has real spatial texture. But implement those ideas in a standard neural network and that texture collapses into one global loss. The [Free Energy Principle](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn2787) tells a similar story: rich framework, single number in practice.

A radio is a good way to see why this matters. Static produces sound and a station produces sound. If all you measured was total volume, they'd look identical. But the signals are completely different - one is energy scattered randomly across all frequencies, the other is organized at a specific frequency carrying actual information. You can't distinguish them by loudness. You distinguish them by looking at the structure.

My bet is that prediction error works the same way. Inside a settling system, noise and genuine novelty produce measurably different disturbance geometries. The scalar throws that geometry away before anyone gets to use it.

## Surprise has shape

What would that geometry actually look like?

Think about walking into a crowded party where everyone is talking over each other. A lot of acoustic energy hits your auditory system, but none of it grabs you. It washes over everything without forcing any specific part of your processing into a new configuration.

Now imagine a single unfamiliar voice across the room says something that makes you turn your head. That's a different kind of event. It recruits a specific part of your processing, forces it into unfamiliar territory while everything else stays stable, and takes longer to resolve because there's something there to make sense of.

Both events can register as the same total activation. But the shape of the disturbance is completely different.

Now picture a system with a thousand interacting units. A random perturbation nudges all of them a little - weak activation everywhere, and it dies out fast. A novel but structured input does something else: it lights up a tight cluster of units, keeps them active across many settling steps, and concentrates energy in a few coherent modes. Same total disturbance, but the geometry is nothing alike.

**Surprise has shape.**

It has a spatial dimension: which parts of the system got disturbed and which stayed stable.

It has a temporal dimension: did the disturbance wash out immediately, or did the system take time to settle through intermediate states.

And it has a spectral dimension: were the excited modes high-frequency and scattered, or low-frequency and coherent.

Spatial tells you *where* to look. Temporal tells you *how deep* it goes. Spectral tells you whether you're looking at noise or structure.

## From feedforward to settling

The reason nobody has used this is that the dominant substrate doesn't produce it. A [feedforward network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedforward_neural_network) takes an input, runs it through layers, and gives you an output. No settling, no trajectory through intermediate states, no way to read the geometry of surprise off the computation itself. Researchers have built software workarounds - [ensemble methods](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.06505) that use model disagreement as a proxy for uncertainty, [Bayesian surprise](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S037015732300203X) that measures belief shifts - but these all reconstruct shape from the outside rather than observing it directly.

A thermodynamic substrate changes this. If computation happens through settling - a physical process where the system relaxes toward a solution over time - then surprise shows up in the trajectory itself. The spatial, temporal, and spectral signatures are there, readable, as the system does its ordinary work.

Once you can observe that structure directly, the system can start to organize its own learning. Think about how children develop - a toddler doesn't try to learn calculus, and they don't stare at random static. They're drawn to things at the edge of what they can currently understand, slightly novel but structured enough to learn from. Developmental psychologists call this the [Goldilocks zone](http://www.pyoudeyer.com/oudeyerGottliebLopesPBR16.pdf), and children navigate toward it naturally, building their own curriculum without anyone telling them what to study next.

A system that can read the shape of its own surprise could do the same thing, self-selecting what's worth engaging with and building complexity over time the way a child does. It could also protect what it already knows. If consolidated regions are stable and new surprise is hitting somewhere else, there's no reason to risk overwriting old knowledge. Catastrophic forgetting stops being a global threat and becomes a local question.

## HAWF

This is what I'm building. HAWF - Hierarchical Associative Wave Fields. I wrote more about the architecture in my [last post](https://www.sethmorton.com/blog/what_you_attend_to_cannot_be_static), but the core idea is that learning and inference come from the same physical process: the system gets perturbed, it settles, and the settling is the computation.

Concretely: HAWF is an energy-based model where nodes sit in a hierarchical landscape and interact through learned couplings. When a new input arrives, it perturbs the landscape and the system relaxes toward a new attractor. During that relaxation, you can read which nodes moved and which stayed put (spatial), how long the system took to converge and whether it passed through metastable states (temporal), and which frequency modes got excited in the process (spectral). A transformer gives you a single forward pass and a loss. HAWF gives you a settling trajectory you can decompose.

The shape of surprise is a native observable in this substrate, not something you bolt on after the fact with a reward head. That doesn't mean the problem is solved, because you still need learning rules that know what to do with those measurements. But the measurements are there, in the physics.

The question shifts. Instead of asking how to build a better scalar reward for curiosity, you can ask: what did the disturbance look like, what does that geometry mean, and what part of the system should change because of it?

Surprise has shape. And I think that shape is where continual learning actually begins.
