
### Biotech has a distribution problem

My best friend, at 20 years old, has two genetic diseases with no cure.

It's a big part of why I got into biotech.

And the deeper I've gone, the more I've noticed something frustrating: there's so much promising work in biotech, but it doesn't spread. Results get published, tools get built, and then they just... sit there. They don't reach the people who could actually use them or build on them.

It’s tempting to say the gap is money, or regulation, or, put simply: “biology is hard.”

All of these are true. But I keep coming back to something simpler:

**Biotech has a distribution problem.**

I mean distribution in two senses:

1. The distribution of *participation*: the pathways for outsiders to take on meaningful work in biotech are too narrow. Too credentialed, too slow-moving, too socially restricted.
    
2. The distribution of *work*: Biotech’s work doesn’t spread outside of insular communities. Breakthroughs and press releases don’t reliably reach people who could collaborate and build on top of them.

I want to share why biotech doesn't travel, why this matters, and why biotech needs more people pushing the frontier of AI along with more legitimate on-ramps for young people. And finally, what can we do about it?

First, why

### Biotech Doesn’t Travel 


I have a friend whose dream is to make it possible for anyone to do groundbreaking biological research, using intuitive software and cloud labs.

It's an amazing vision. I can picture someone running experiments in their bedroom the way they can release software today.

But even if they build something incredible, the hard part starts after the breakthrough.

**Because biotech doesn’t travel.** 

Let me explain:

In software, when something gains traction, distribution can compound rapidly. People try it, share it, remix it, complain about it, and recruit themselves into your orbit.

Biotech is the opposite. Results get written up for insiders. Product updates are shaped by IP concerns, and compliance requirements. News comes filtered through a handful of academic press cycles, venture capitalists making posts, or niche communities.

This matters because if people can't evaluate your work and figure out how to attach to it, it can't compound as easily.

When work is visible, other scientists can build on it. Adjacent fields can connect their insights to yours. Talented people can find you. Funders can understand what you're doing. In biotech, most good work just [sits there, underused](https://www.cwts.nl/TvR/documents/AvR-SlBeau-Scientom.pdf), until someone bigger rediscovers it years later.

This creates a paradox: some of the most important work on Earth is functionally invisible to the people who could accelerate it. And that invisibility has downstream consequences. It's not just that good work stays hidden; it's that the people who could build on that work never see it in the first place. Which brings me to the first gap in biotech participation:

### Biotech needs more frontier AI people. 

Biotech oscillates between [two modes](https://x.com/SynBio1/status/1998454536558575958?s=20): intuition-driven lab cycles with low-information dense signals, and what I call the “silicon maximalist” approach, which tries to simulate everything in silicon before we have the data, compute, or models to do it right.

The near future of biotech is closed-loop: experiments run at high throughput, designed to extract maximum useful information, paired with machine learning models that learn continuously. Models suggest the next experiments to run, labs execute them, and an inference layer converts raw experimental data into signals the model can learn from. The results feed back into the model, the model learns, and the cycle repeats. Humans guide the process by making strategic decisions about what to explore and defining constraints . If you’re familiar with [reinforcement learning](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/deep-learning-nutshell-reinforcement-learning/), it’s a similar shape: the system improves through ongoing interaction with the real world, not just from training once on static data.

I’ve noticed a lot of computational biology treats progress as getting more labels and getting better at predicting them. This has been useful, but it quietly assumes the labels we’re collecting are the right compression of reality, and that they stay meaningful across contexts. In biology, this often fails. Labels are usually proxies, and the hard part is noticing when you’re optimizing the proxy instead of what you actually care about, or when the thing that matters isn’t in your measurement set at all. This creates a big blind spot for [“unknown unknowns.”](https://sketchplanations.com/unknown-unknowns) The biggest wins will come from loops that help us discover better targets, better measurements, and better proxies, not just better labels.

I'm a massive fan of JURA bio's approach here: [manufacturing-aware design](http://jura.bio) where AI & biology are deeply coupled. 

This is a biology and AI problem in the same way modern robotics is a mechanical engineering and AI problem.

The challenge is that most AI experts don't deeply understand biology, and most biologists don't deeply understand frontier AI. Bridging this gap requires both sides to meet in the middle, which means biotech companies need to actively recruit frontier AI talent and genuinely integrate them into the work. If biotech remains invisible and insular, these people will simply build something else instead (right now, mostly LLMs).

To be clear: I'm not arguing for replacing biologists. Rather, I want biotech labs to integrate frontier AI capabilities so they can move faster than traditional wet lab cycles allow. Posts like [this](https://www.jura.bio/blog/onebillionexperiments) further exemplify why the high-information-density closed-loop approach is so impactful.

Once again, this is a distribution problem. The work that most needs frontier AI remains illegible to those who could contribute it. And when some do find their way in, they encounter narrow gates and an implicit message that you only matter after years of credentialing.

Nowhere is that more obvious than with young people.

This leads me into my next point.

### Biotech Needs More Young People

Biotech has a permissioning problem.

In biotech, the default filter is credentials, not output. There's a version of this that's responsible caution. Biology is hard. Mistakes can hurt people. Regulatory environments are complex. But these claims overlook opportunity cost: delays hurt people too.

Let me elaborate:

If it takes a decade just to be allowed into the room, the room selects for a narrow kind of person: people who can afford long credential marathons, people who can tolerate long stretches of low pay, and people whose lives eventually make high-risk bets harder. This i measurable: the median time from finishing a PhD to receiving a first major NIH research grant doubled from 6 years in 1990 to 12 years in [2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8011905/). That’s twelve years before most people get real independence to pick and pursue their own bets.

Biotech has historically dismissed young people with a familiar refrain: "Biology is too complex for newcomers – you need decades of training before you can contribute meaningfully." This is partly true. But the slope has changed. Papers, textbooks, and whole subfields are now easier to digest with LLMs. More importantly, there are many ways to contribute that don’t require you to run clinical trials: building tools, automating workflows, improving measurement, mapping literature, creating better evaluation loops, etc. 

If we keep requiring everyone to credential for a decade before they can matter, we’ll keep selecting out the people most willing to try new approaches, early.

### So What Can We Do About It?

Here are a couple of thoughts on how we can begin to fix this. 

#### Biotech in plain English


Imagine a world where you can send a paper to a friend (from outside the biotech world), and they’ll be just as amazed as you are.

E.G.

Instead of:

> During training, we apply a noising schedule over timesteps T that gradually corrupts antibody residue frames toward a noise distribution. We add 3D Gaussian noise to the C-alpha translations and apply Brownian motion on the space of 3D rotations for the orientations. We then train RFdiffusion to predict the denoised structure at each timestep by minimizing the mean squared error between the clean frames X0 and the network’s predicted frames. (this is a real article, here's the link: [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10983868/))

Perhaps something like this:

> During training, we intentionally add controlled random shifts and rotations to the antibody backbone and teach the model to remove that corruption one step at a time. At generation time, this learned “reverse process” lets the model start from noise and iteratively refine it into a realistic antibody structure consistent with the constraints we provide.

Imagine if every paper, every product launch, every company update had two versions:
- the real technical one      
- and a plain English one that’s easier to understand  

When people can understand something, they can support it, join it, fund it, argue about it, and improve it.

#### Build/Research in public


I really appreciated this [tweet](https://x.com/shelbynewsad/status/1991546244263489603) from Dr. Shelby on biotech’s lack of narrative. Here’s my own spin on it:

Biotech often operates in silos. Researchers work on similar problems without seeing what’s happening in adjacent labs or subfields. We need more low-friction connection points: online spaces where people share work in progress in plain English, across specialty boundaries, plus more communicators translating advances for everyone else. 

The plain English part matters. Biology is huge, and the the "jargon surface area" is basically infinite.

And to everyone already writing and podcasting about biotech: keep going! You're making the work visible when it needs to be.

#### Default to curiosity before critique.

In tech, when someone shows you a something new, the default reaction is curiosity: "If this worked, what could it unlock?" In biotech, the default is critique: "Here are all the reasons this won't work." Critique is important, but it can't be the only response. We need more people asking "How could this work?" instead of just explaining why it won't. This is how you create space for the orthogonal approaches that outsiders bring.

The next era of biotech (and frankly, most industries) will come from teams that combine the frontier of different domains, make their work legible enough that the right people can find it, and build on-ramps that make those people actually want to join.
